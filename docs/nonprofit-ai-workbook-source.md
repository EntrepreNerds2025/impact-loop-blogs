## Week 1, Assess: what is this work actually doing?

The hardest part of nonprofit AI adoption is not technical. It is honest.

Most nonprofits skip Assess because the question feels obvious. Comms is comms, right? Reports are reports. Donor emails are donor emails. We know what we do.

You don't, actually. Not in the resolution that matters for AI decisions. The Assess phase forces a higher resolution: every recurring piece of communication your org produces gets sorted into one of three jobs. The job determines whether AI belongs anywhere near it.

### The three jobs

**Job 1, Operational.** The reader doesn't care who wrote it. They care that it is accurate and clear. Internal team docs. FAQ pages. Meeting notes. Vendor briefs. Process documentation. Onboarding emails for new contractors. Foundation acknowledgement receipts. SOPs. The team's mid-month status update to itself. AI is good at this work. Sometimes better than a tired human who would otherwise rush it.

**Job 2, Informational.** The reader cares the information is correct and the explanation is clear. They mostly do not care who wrote it. Blog posts explaining a program. How-to guides for community members. Plain-language summaries of research. Press release drafts. Service descriptions on the website. AI can do most of the work here, but a human should still rewrite the voice-heavy parts. Use AI as co-writer, not as the writer.

**Job 3, Relational.** The reader is paying attention to whether the voice still sounds like a person who cares. Donor appeal letters. Grant reports to your largest funders. The story of why your org exists. Sensitive community communications. Personal thank-you notes. The newsletter that goes to your community. AI cannot do this work. Not because the model isn't smart enough, because the reader can tell. The second they can tell, the trust is gone.

### Why the sort matters

The nonprofits that get AI adoption right run AI on Operational and most of Informational. They do not let AI touch Relational. Ever. Even when the team feels overwhelmed and someone says "let's just use ChatGPT for the year-end appeal." That is the moment AI breaks the brand.

Most nonprofits running AI badly right now have it backwards. They feel most overwhelmed by the Relational work, so they reach for AI exactly where it cannot help. The team feels productive for two weeks, donations soften, and nobody connects the dots until the next quarterly review.

Sort the work first. Decide where AI is allowed second.

### Worksheet: Sort your work

List every recurring piece of communication your org produces. Go through the last 90 days of email outputs, blog posts, social, reports, donor mail, board memos. Aim for at least 25 items.

For each one, mark which job it does:

| Communication | Frequency | Audience | Job (Op / Info / Rel) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Example: Year-end donor appeal email | Annual | Donors | Relational |
| Example: M&E summary in funder report | Quarterly | Foundation officers | Relational |
| Example: Service description on website | Ongoing | Search visitors | Informational |
| Example: Internal weekly team update | Weekly | Internal | Operational |
| _Add your rows below_ |  |  |  |

When you finish: count how many rows are in each column. Most nonprofits are surprised. The Relational column is usually the smallest by row count but the largest by trust impact. The Operational column is usually larger than expected, and that is where AI can free up real capacity without risk.

This is the foundation for everything in Week 2. Take it seriously. The honest sort is the whole game.

## Week 2, Discover: where does AI actually fit?

You now have a sorted inventory. Week 2 narrows it.

The Discover phase is about deciding which one specific outcome you will build toward in Week 3, and matching the work to a tool category that genuinely fits.

### Five places AI consistently fits for nonprofits

**1. Inbox triage.** A small AI agent that classifies incoming email and routes it to the right place. Donor inquiry, grant deadline, vendor invoice, internal, partnership, community member. No drafting, no replying, just routing. Saves the executive director or operations lead 4-7 hours a week of inbox archaeology. Low risk because nothing goes out.

**2. Document summarization.** Foundation reports come in long. Board packets come in long. Research comes in long. AI summarization is genuinely good at compressing structured documents into the 3-5 bullet points the team actually needs. Use this for incoming documents, not outgoing.

**3. Internal documentation drafting.** SOPs, onboarding docs, runbooks, training materials. AI produces structured first drafts in minutes. The team rewrites the parts that need org-specific context. Saves real authoring time on operational documentation.

**4. Data extraction from PDFs and CRM exports.** Pulling stats, dates, names, amounts out of structured documents into spreadsheet form. Classification work. Deterministic-shaped. Low risk if validated with a sample.

**5. First-pass grant research.** AI can scan grant databases, summarize fit by criteria, and produce a shortlist. The decision-maker still has to evaluate fit and write the proposal. This is research-acceleration, not application-writing.

### Five places AI does not fit for nonprofits

**1. Donor relationship layer.** Personal thank-you notes. Appeal emails. The story of a beneficiary. The voice has to be yours.

**2. Sensitive community communications.** Conversations with families in crisis. Communications to youth in the program. Anything in the community-organizer lane where tone-deafness has real consequences.

**3. Brand-defining narrative.** The "about us" page, the founder's story, the mission statement, the homepage hero copy. These are the places people decide whether to trust you. Write them yourself.

**4. Sensitive grant work.** Multi-year capacity grant reports, foundation transition narratives, anything where the funder is reading for nuance and partnership signal. Generic phrasing in these reports kills next-cycle funding faster than missing a deadline.

**5. Stakeholder communication during a crisis.** Reputational issue, leadership transition, program closure, community incident. AI generates the wrong texture for these moments. Slow down, write it yourself, send it.

### The Discover criteria

For the workflow you are considering automating, ask:

1. Which job does it belong to? (Operational / Informational / Relational)
2. Where does the time currently go? (drafting / reviewing / coordinating)
3. What would change for the team if this workflow took half the time?
4. What is the failure cost if the AI gets it wrong once? (low / medium / high)
5. Can a human review the output before it goes anywhere external?

Workflows that are Operational or Informational, where time mostly goes to drafting, where the team would meaningfully benefit from time savings, where the failure cost is low to medium, and where a human review gate is already in place, those are AI-fit. Pick one of these for Week 3.

## Week 3, Apply: build the smallest version that could work

Apply is where most nonprofit AI adoption fails, not because the tools don't work, but because teams overscope the first version.

Build the smallest possible thing that could produce the outcome from Week 2. Use the simplest possible tool. Resist every urge to make it impressive on the first pass.

### Two failure modes to avoid

**The committee.** Five people, three meetings, no shipped version. Apply is owned by one person. That person decides what to build, builds it, and reports back. Other team members get involved only at the review gate.

**The vendor.** A SaaS tool gets bought. Onboarding takes three weeks. Customization takes two more. Output is generic. Quietly stops getting used. Apply uses the simplest tool that could possibly work, usually that's a generic chat interface (Claude, ChatGPT, or whichever your org has access to) with a saved prompt template, not a category-specific SaaS.

### Three sample build patterns

**Pattern A, Saved prompt template.** For document summarization, drafting, or categorization workflows. The operator writes a prompt template that takes a structured input (the document, the email, the data export) and produces a structured output (the summary, the categorization, the draft). The template lives in a shared doc. The operator runs it manually at first, sees the output quality, refines the prompt over the week. Total build time: 1-3 hours. Total tool cost: ~$20/month for the AI subscription.

**Pattern B, Routing automation.** For inbox triage. The operator wires Gmail / Outlook to a no-code platform (Zapier, Make.com, n8n) that runs the email through a classification prompt and routes to the right channel. Total build time: 4-8 hours. Total tool cost: ~$30-100/month including AI.

**Pattern C, Spreadsheet-bound workflow.** For data extraction or research. The operator builds a Google Sheet with a column that triggers an AI prompt on each new row. Inputs go in one column, outputs land in the next. Total build time: 2-5 hours. Total tool cost: ~$20/month.

For the v1, pick the simplest pattern that fits your workflow. Resist the urge to build something more sophisticated.

### What to log

The v1 must produce its work AND a log of what it did. Date. Input. Output. Time elapsed. Whether the output was used as-is, edited, or thrown away. This log is what Week 4 evaluates. Without it, you have no way to compare with-AI to without-AI honestly.

## Week 4, Produce + Transform: compare honestly, hand off cleanly

Week 4 has two jobs. Run the comparison that determines whether the v1 is worth keeping (Produce). Convert what works into a system the team owns (Transform).

### Produce: the three measurements

Run the v1 alongside the manual process for 2-4 weeks of real work. Track three things:

**Time.** How long does the workflow take with AI, end-to-end including review? How long did it take without AI? Be honest about review time. AI that saves 30 minutes of drafting but adds 45 minutes of review is a net loss.

**Quality.** Is the output as good as what the team was producing manually? This is where you run the Trust Test (next page). A v1 that fails the Trust Test for any output category that touches external audiences gets retired.

**Team energy.** Does the operator feel more capable or more drained? AI that technically saves time but burns out the operator is a net loss. Ask the operator at the end of each week. Their answer is the data.

A v1 that wins on all three graduates to Transform. A v1 that fails on any of the three either gets redesigned (one more cycle of Apply) or retired.

### The Trust Test

Take a sample of the AI's output. Hand it to one person from your audience without telling them how it was produced. Ask them whether anything reads off.

For Operational workflows, "audience" means a different team member or a partner. The bar is lower: does the output communicate clearly?

For Informational workflows, "audience" means a community member or stakeholder who reads your content. The bar is medium: does the output sound like the org's voice?

For workflows that touch Relational work (which should be rare), "audience" means a donor, board member, or major stakeholder. The bar is high: do they spot the AI?

If they spot the AI, the output is not ready for that workflow. No exceptions.

### Anti-patterns to retire

If your v1 has any of these, retire it:

**It depends on a single team member knowing the prompt.** Tribal knowledge that disappears when the person leaves. Document the prompt, share it, version it.

**Nobody is reviewing the output before it goes external.** AI-without-review is the failure mode that breaks trust the fastest. Even the simplest review gate (one person eyeballing the output for 30 seconds before sending) prevents the worst outcomes.

**The output sounds the same regardless of input.** This is a sign the prompt is too generic. Real workflows produce different outputs for different inputs. If your AI keeps writing the same email, the prompt needs more context-specific structure.

**The team has stopped using it but nobody's said so.** AI workflows abandoned in silence accumulate. Ask the operator directly at week 4: "Are you still using this?" If the answer is "not really," retire it on the spot.

### Transform: hand off cleanly

A v1 that survives Produce becomes a system the team owns. Transform documents four things:

**1. The runbook.** Step-by-step documentation of the workflow. If a new team member could not run the workflow from this document, the runbook is not done. Include the prompt, the tool, the inputs, the outputs, the review steps.

**2. The maintenance plan.** What happens when the AI tool changes pricing, the prompt stops working, the API key rotates, or the operator leaves the org. One page. Most nonprofits skip this and end up with abandoned AI workflows when the original builder moves on.

**3. The tone-of-voice gate.** Specifically for any Informational workflow that touches external audiences. A documented checklist of what the output must sound like before it gets sent. Catches drift over time.

**4. The budget.** What does this workflow cost per month in tool fees, API calls, and team time? Most nonprofits answer this question never. Knowing the number protects you when the next budget cycle starts.

## Closing: what to do next

You have run one ADAPT cycle. Congratulations. The next cycle picks one more workflow from your Week 1 inventory and runs the same five phases.

Most nonprofits we work with run their first three cycles in roughly the same order:

**Cycle 1:** an Operational workflow (often inbox triage or document summarization). Builds team confidence. Low risk. Genuine time savings.

**Cycle 2:** an Informational workflow (often an internal docs drafting workflow or a research-acceleration workflow). Demonstrates that AI can support content work without compromising voice.

**Cycle 3:** a higher-leverage Operational workflow that the team now feels ready to tackle (often donor data hygiene, M&E reporting, or grant tracking).

After three cycles, most teams stop needing facilitation. They have the framework, they have a few working systems, and they pick the next workflow themselves.

## Appendix: quick-reference templates

### Sample Operational prompt, meeting notes summarizer

```
You are summarizing internal meeting notes for [ORG NAME].
The audience is the leadership team. The output goes in the weekly internal update.

Input: meeting transcript pasted below
Output: structured summary with three sections:
  1. Decisions made (bullet list)
  2. Action items with owner and deadline (table)
  3. Open questions for next meeting (bullet list)

Keep the tone direct. Skip filler. If a decision is unclear, flag it as "needs follow-up" rather than guessing.

Transcript: [PASTE]
```

### Sample Informational prompt, program FAQ drafter

```
You are drafting an FAQ section for [ORG NAME]'s [PROGRAM NAME] page.
The audience is community members considering applying to the program.

Input: program description and existing comms about the program (pasted below)
Output: 8-10 plain-language FAQ items with direct answers.

Voice: warm, clear, no jargon. Avoid corporate-speak. Avoid superlatives.
Match the existing comms voice, read what's pasted before writing.

If you don't have enough information to answer a question accurately, write [NEEDS CONFIRMATION] in place of the answer.

Inputs: [PASTE]
```

### Tone-of-voice gate, sample checklist

Before any AI-assisted output goes external, run through:

- [ ] Does this sound like our org, or like every other org?
- [ ] Are there any specific phrases the team would never use?
- [ ] Does it name specific things (people, places, programs, dates) where it should?
- [ ] Does it avoid superlatives that aren't earned?
- [ ] Would a long-time supporter recognize this as ours?

If any answer is no, edit before sending.
